Abstract: Skill libraries in deployed robotic systems are continually up-dated through fine-tuning, fresh demonstrations, or domain adaptation, yet existing typed-composition methods (BLADE, SymSkill, Generative Skill Chaining) treat the library as frozen at test time and offer no analysis of how composition outcomes change when an underlying skill is replaced. We introduce a paired-sampling cross-version swap protocol on standard robosuite manipulation tasks and characterize this previously unstudied dimension of compositional skill learning. On a representative dual-arm peg-in-hole task we discover a dominant-skill effect : a single ECM in the candidate set achieves 86 .7% atomic success rate while every other ECM is at or below 26 .7% , and whether this dominant ECM is included in a composition shifts the success rate by up to +50 percentage points. We characterize the boundary of the effect on a simpler pick task where all atomic policies saturate at 100% and the effect is consequently undefined. Across three independent tasks we further find that off-policy behavioral distance metrics fail to identify the dominant ECM, ruling out the most natural cheap predictor. Building on these observations, we propose an atomic-quality probe and a Hybrid Selector that combines per-skill probes (zero per-decision cost) with selective composition revalidation (full cost), and characterize its Pareto frontier on 144 skill-update decisions. On T6 the atomic-only probe sits 23 pp below full revalidation ( 64 .6% vs 87 .5% oracle match) at zero per-decision cost, but a Hybrid Selector with margin m= 10

1 Introduction 

Deployed robotic skill libraries are not static. The arrival of generalist policies explicitly designed to be fine-tuned [ 1, 2 , 3], the release of large-scale heterogeneous robot datasets [ 4, 5 ], and the broader vision-language-action wave [ 6] make post-deployment skill updates—through fine-tuning, fresh demonstrations, RLHF, or domain adaptation—a routine event rather than an exceptional one. Yet the recent typed-composition literature for robot manipulation [ 7, 8 , 9]treats the skill library as fixed at test time, and the adjacent “open-ended skill library” line of work [ 10 , 11 , 12 ] grows the library but does not study what happens when an existing skill is replaced . The question of how composition outcomes change when one of the constituent skills is later updated has, to our knowledge, not been formulated as an empirical research question, even though such replacements are precisely what generalist policies were built to support. This paper closes that gap. We ask: when an existing skill in a compositional library is replaced by an independently-trained alternative, what happens to the compositions that depend on it? We treat this as a measurement question first and an algorithmic one second. We introduce a paired-sampling cross-version swap protocol that varies the version of one or more phase ECMs while holding all other factors fixed. Our findings on robosuite manipulation are fourfold. (1) The population-average effect of a swap on composition success is statistically zero, yet cell-level variance inflates 1.14 –2.54 ×

across the four phases—an apparently null finding that masks substantial structure. (2) On the dual-arm peg-in-hole task, that variance is explained by a dominant-skill effect : a single ECM in the candidate set drives composition outcomes, and swapping it shifts the success rate by up to 50 percentage points. (3) On a simpler pick task all candidate ECMs saturate at 100% atomic success rate and the effect is consequently undefined—a clean boundary that delineates when the effect can manifest. (4) On three tasks, behavioral distance fails to identify the dominant ECM, ruling out the natural cheap predictor. Building on these, we propose an atomic-quality probe —each candidate ECM evaluated alone—and a Hybrid Selector combining atomic probes with selective composition revalidation; on 144 update decisions, atomic-only is within 3 percentage points of full revalidation at zero per-decision cost (under a mixed-oracle caveat we analyze in detail). Our contributions are: (i) the paired-sampling cross-version swap protocol; (ii) empirical characterization of the dominant-skill effect on a positive task and its boundary on a saturated task; (iii) the atomic-probe Hybrid Selector for skill-update governance, with cost–quality trade-off on 144 decisions across three tasks.

Skill libraries and skill chaining. A complementary line treats robotic competence as a library of reusable modules. Voyager [ 10 ], BOSS [ 11 ], and LOTUS [ 12 ] are append-only : they grow the library at deployment without removing or updating existing skills. SayCan [ 15 ] and Code-as-Policies [ 16 ] pair language-model planning with primitive skill calls. T-STAR [ 17 ]addresses the closely related problem of skill chaining when individual skills’ terminal-state distributions disagree, using terminal-state regularization at training time. Skill priors and hierarchical RL [ 18 , 19 , 20 , 21 ] provide the methodological foundation on which our SAC-trained ECMs sit. None of this literature, to our knowledge, formalizes the question of what happens to existing compositions when one of the constituent skills is later updated. 

Generalist policies, evaluation, and continual learning. Vision–language–action models such as OpenVLA [ 1 ], Octo [ 2], π0 [3 ], and RT-2 [ 6], together with the Open X-Embodiment / RT-X collaboration’s large heterogeneous datasets [ 5 ] and the DROID in-the-wild dataset [ 4], are explicitly designed for downstream fine-tuning, making post-deployment skill updates a routine event. Recent benchmarks evaluate such generalist policies in simulation [ 22 ] and in distributed real-world setups [ 23 ], but these evaluate policies as monolithic units rather than the post-update composition stability we target. Imitation-learning ECM architectures [ 24 , 25 ] are also candidates for the present protocol but are out of scope here. The broader continual-learning literature on catastrophic forgetting [ 26 ]addresses how to update a single neural function while retaining old behavior; our question is complementary: given that an update has occurred, what happens to compositions built on top of the updated function? 

Compositional benchmarks. CompoSuite [ 27 ], LIBERO [ 28 , 29 ], ClevrSkills [ 30 ], and CALVIN [ 31 ] probe compositional generalization to novel tasks or compositions. None evaluates an existing composition under evolving skill modules. We follow standard task definitions from robosuite [ 32 ] but introduce a swap-based evaluation protocol for that previously unstudied dimension. 

3 Setup 

3.1 Compositional Skill Execution 

Following the spirit of Liu et al. [7] , Shao et al. [8] , we represent a long-horizon manipulation task as an ordered sequence of phases Π = ( π1, . . . , π K ), each phase served by a phase-specific neural controller—an Embodied Capability Module (ECM). At runtime, ECM ck is invoked on entry to phase πk and is replaced by ck+1 on a phase-termination predicate. Whereas BLADE and SymSkill extract task-specific phase schedules from demonstrations or LLMs, we use a fixed K=4 decomposition Π = ( reach , grasp , lift , place ) for experimental tractability. A 

composition is an assignment C = ( c1, . . . , c K ) with each ck drawn from a pool of candidate ECMs trained for phase πk.33.2 Evaluated Tasks 

We evaluate on six tasks from the robosuite manipulation benchmark [ 32 ]: T1_Pick, T2_Place, T3_Stack, T4_NutAssembly, T5_PickPlaceMulti, and T6_TwoArmPegInHole. Each task is decomposed into the same four phases ( reach , grasp , lift , place ), with phase-specific ECMs trained from scratch using SAC. The dominant-skill effect (section 4) is observable only on tasks where (a) atomic ECMs achieve non-trivial task success and (b) atomic quality varies across versions. Among our six tasks this holds only for T6_TwoArmPegInHole (dual-arm contact-rich peg insertion); on T1_Pick all atomic ECMs saturate at 100% (boundary case, section 5); on T2–T5 all atomic ECMs score 0% under our standard 50K ×20 -iteration SAC schedule (discussed in section 9). We use T6 for the dominant-skill effect and T1 for the boundary; for the behavioral-distance refutation (section 6) and the algorithm benchmark (section 7) we additionally use T3 and T4, on which the oracle is necessarily defined on episode reward rather than task success—a methodological limitation we keep visible throughout.

4 The Dominant-Skill Effect 

A naive expectation is that swapping one phase’s ECM for an independently-trained sibling will perturb composition outcomes one way or the other. At the level of population mean this expectation fails: across the four phases of T6, paired t-tests on the full 4 × 4 cross-seed swap matrices yield p-values of 0.385 (reach ), 0.616 (grasp ), 0.790 (lift ), ≈ 1.0 (place )—no average effect (no multiple-comparison correction applied; all p > 0.3 uncorrected). Yet the cell-level variance of composition success rate inflates under swap by 1.14 × to 2.54 × across the four phases relative to the within-version diagonal, indicating structured rather than random perturbation. We show below that some swaps help while others hurt in a way that cancels in expectation: one specific ECM in the candidate set is disproportionately responsible for composition success, and the sign of any swap is determined by whether this dominant ECM enters or leaves the composition. 5 Boundary: When Atomic Quality Saturates 

The dominant-skill effect described in Section 4 requires variation in atomic skill quality across versions: some ECMs must be markedly stronger than others. We characterize the boundary of this effect using a task on which all atomic ECMs are equally strong. On T1_Pick, a single-arm pick of one object from a fixed table position, every (seed, phase) ECM in our library achieves q(c) = 100% atomic success rate (table 4). All four seeds saturate from the first training iteration and hold 100% across 15 iterations of the standard schedule; on the N =30 atomic-probe evaluation, every one of the 4 seeds × 4 phases = 16 

cells reports 100% success ( 120 /120 episodes). The candidate set is uniformly saturated; there is no dominant cell. By the mechanism of section 4, when every candidate ECM contributes equally well, every composition is equally successful and swapping any phase’s ECM cannot shift outcomes. 7 Algorithm: Atomic-Quality Probing for Skill-Update Governance 

7.1 Problem Formulation 

A skill-update event is a tuple (p, a, π k) specifying that the phase-πk ECM in primary version 

p is candidate to be replaced by the phase-πk ECM from version a. A selector is a function 

S : ( p, a, π k) → { accept , reject } that decides whether to apply the update. We compare each selector against the oracle selector that has post-hoc access to the true paired composition success rate of the post-update composition C′ and accepts iff P( success |

C′) ≥ P(success | C) − τ for a tolerance threshold τ (we report results for τ = 5 pp). Oracle match is the fraction of update events where a selector’s decision agrees with the oracle.

The dominant-skill effect is then a downstream consequence of this robustness asymmetry, related to the broader observation that fine-tuning can distort pretrained features on out-of-distribution inputs [ 35 ]. This also explains why the population-mean swap effect vanishes: within a candidate set, swapping toward the dominant ECM (when the primary lacks it) and away from it produce gains and losses of comparable magnitude; the signal lives in the conditional structure, not the marginal mean. A “naive” methodology that reports population-mean ∆ under swap will systematically miss the phenomenon. 

Alternative mechanisms. Several refinements of the robustness-asymmetry story are testable in the current data. (a) Hand-off state coverage: the dominant ECM may simply visit a wider region of phase-end states, so any downstream phase finds itself in-distribution. 

(b) Action smoothness: smoother action trajectories reduce contact discontinuities at phase transitions, an effect related to T-STAR’s terminal-state regularization [ 17 ]. (c) Trajectory-length distribution: a dominant ECM may finish its phase faster (or slower) on average, leaving the downstream phase a larger time budget. We do not adjudicate among these in this paper, but each yields a falsifiable prediction on phase-end state statistics that the released subset-swap data support testing in future work. 

Connection to typed-composition literature and deployment. Our findings reinforce the typed-composition thread [ 7, 8, 9] that pre/post- condition structure is the right level at which to reason about compositional behavior. Where prior work stops at constructing such structure, we add a complementary observation: even with identical type signatures, two ECM versions can produce dramatically different composition outcomes, and the difference is captured by an atomic-quality probe rather than by any structural metric. Concretely for deployment: every candidate skill update should be probed atomically first ; composition probes (far more expensive) should be invoked only when the atomic margin is insufficient. 

9 Limitations and Future Work 

Single positive task. The dominant-skill effect is demonstrated on a single task (T6_TwoArmPegInHole). The boundary case (T1_Pick) and the behavioral-distance refu-tation across three tasks (T6, T3, T4) buttress but do not multiply the positive evidence. We attempted to add a second positive case by training T2_Place under a 30 -iteration scout protocol with intermediate atomic-probe evaluation; all 24 (checkpoint, phase) cells achieved 0% atomic success, so we aborted the attempt. A larger task suite in which atomic policies can be reliably trained to non-saturated success rates would strengthen the empirical support. 9Atomic training as a confounder. Four of our six candidate robosuite tasks (T2–T5) reached 0% atomic success under our standard SAC schedule. The dominant-skill effect is consequently undefined on those tasks; we do not claim the effect is absent , only that the schedule does not yield a measurable candidate set. Whether longer or otherwise-modified atomic training recovers the effect on these tasks is an open question, and the dominant ECM on T6 is identified at S=4 which should be read as “the highest-quality ECM” rather than a strict combinatorial claim; larger S would likely reveal a continuum of quality with the same predictive structure. 

Mixed-oracle algorithm benchmark; idealized update model. The cross-task al-gorithm comparison (table 7) uses a success-rate oracle on T6 but a reward oracle on T3 and T4 out of necessity, so its average should be read as suggestive. The cross-version swap protocol itself simulates update via independent retraining; realistic continual-learning updates (fine-tuning, RLHF, domain adaptation) likely produce smoother version shifts and a different distribution of ∆q, on which the same atomic-probe primitive should still apply but with different effect sizes. 

Single embodiment. All experiments use the same robosuite Panda arm. Cross-embodiment composition stability is an open direction. 

10 Conclusion 

We have characterized composition stability under skill-update events in compositional robot policies. On the dual-arm peg-in-hole task we identified a strong dominant-skill effect: composition outcomes are governed by whether a single, identifiable, high-atomic-quality ECM is included in the composition, and swapping it produces success-rate shifts of up to 

50 percentage points. We characterized the boundary of this effect on a saturated single-arm pick task where the effect is, by construction, undefined. We further showed that off-policy behavioral distance metrics fail to identify the dominant ECM on all three tasks where we tested them, ruling out the most natural cheap predictor. Finally, we proposed an atomic-quality probe and Hybrid Selector that achieve oracle match within 3 percentage points of full composition revalidation at zero or fractional per-decision cost across 144 

update events. Skill libraries in deployment are continually updated; the atomic-quality probe is, to our knowledge, the first principled, deployment-ready primitive for the resulting governance question, and we hope it will encourage further empirical work on composition stability under skill evolution.

Continual learning of skills and policies. Catastrophic forgetting is addressed by elastic-weight consolidation [ 26 ] and a broader incremental-learning taxonomy [ 40 ]: in that taxonomy our cross-version-swap protocol corresponds most closely to task-incremental 

learning where the task identity (the phase) is fixed but the underlying function (the ECM) is replaced. Recent empirical work directly measures forgetting in pretrained robot policies under continual fine-tuning of Octo [ 41 ], the closest empirical sibling to our study at the level of single-policy update. An adjacent line of work treats updates not as replacement but as weight-space merging : Model Soups [ 42 ] averages weights of fine-tuned variants, while Task Arithmetic [ 43 ] edits models via additive task vectors. These approaches keep the library implicitly versioned in weight space; our protocol applies symmetrically to either replacement or merging-based updates, since both ultimately produce a new ECM whose composition stability is the question of interest.
