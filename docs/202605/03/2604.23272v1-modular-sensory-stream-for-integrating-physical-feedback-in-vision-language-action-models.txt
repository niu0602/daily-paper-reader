## Abstract 

Humans understand and interact with the real world by relying on diverse physical feedback be-yond visual perception. Motivated by this, recent approaches attempt to incorporate physical sen-sory signals into Vision-Language-Action mod-els (VLAs). However, they typically focus on a single type of physical signal, failing to capture the heterogeneous and complementary nature of real-world interactions. In this paper, we propose MoSS, a modular sensory stream framework that adapts VLAs to leverage multiple sensory signals for action prediction. Specifically, we introduce decoupled modality streams that integrate hetero-geneous physical signals into the action stream via joint cross-modal self-attention. To enable sta-ble incorporation of new modalities, we adopt a two-stage training scheme that freezes pretrained VLA parameters in the early stage. Furthermore, to better capture contact interaction dynamics, we incorporate an auxiliary task that predicts future physical signals. Through extensive real-world experiments, we demonstrate that MoSS success-fully augments VLAs to leverage diverse physical signals ( i.e., tactile and torque), integrating mul-tiple signals to achieve synergistic performance gains. ## 1. Introduction 

In the real world, humans perform dexterous manipulation by continuously integrating multiple sources of physical sensory feedback that cannot be obtained through visual perception alone. For example, during plug insertion, tactile feedback at the fingertips helps recognize grasp stability, while torque feedback from the arm provides crucial cues for detecting contact and correcting misalignment.

Toward learning human-like generalizable policies, Vision-Language-Action models (VLAs; Zitkovich et al. 2023; Kim et al. 2024; Black et al. 2025a; Huang et al. 2025a; Chen et al. 2025b; Bjorck et al. 2025a; Zheng et al. 2025) have demonstrated remarkable progress by leveraging large-scale pretrained Vision-Language Models (VLMs; Beyer et al. 2024; Bai et al. 2025; Chen et al. 2025a). However, they rely solely on visual observations to generate actions, which fundamentally limits their ability to handle precise and contact-rich manipulation. To mitigate this limitation, recent approaches have ex-plored incorporating a single physical sensory modality into pretrained VLAs, either by encoding it into VLM back-bones (Zhang et al., 2025a; Huang et al., 2025b; Cheng et al., 2025) or by directly conditioning the action expert modules (Yu et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025b). While effec-tive in specific settings, such approaches are not designed to grow with increasing sensory complexity; na ¨ıvely extend-ing them to incorporate multiple physical modalities does not consistently yield complementary performance gains in practice (see Figure 1). This highlights the need for a more general and scalable framework that can effectively inte-grate heterogeneous physical signals for reliable dexterous manipulation. 

Our approach. We present MoSS , a Mo dular Sensory 

Stream framework that seamlessly augments pretrained VLAs to leverage diverse physical sensory signals for ac-tion prediction. Specifically, we append decoupled modality streams to the action expert module, which processes phys-ical inputs and exchanges information with the pretrained parameters via a joint cross-modal self-attention mechanism. To avoid corruption of pretrained parameters by the newly added streams, we introduce a two-stage training scheme. In the first stage, we freeze the pretrained VLA and train only the physical signal streams, allowing them to pre-align their representations with the existing representation space of the pretrained policy. After that, we jointly fine-tune the entire model end-to-end. Furthermore, motivated by Zhang et al. (2025b), we incorporate an auxiliary objective of fu-ture physical signal prediction, which encourages the model to internalize physical interaction dynamics and effectively utilize physical feedback for action generation. 1

## 2. Related Works 

Vision-Language-Action models (VLAs). The develop-ment of generalist robot policies has been fundamentally shaped by the challenge of acquiring diverse, large-scale robotic datasets. While traditional approaches (Shafiul-lah et al., 2022; Chi et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2024) relied on task-specific demonstrations with limited generaliza-tion, VLAs address this bottleneck by leveraging pretrained Vision-Language Models (VLMs) for robotic control. Early VLAs (Zitkovich et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024) gener-ate discretized actions autoregressively as tokens, while more recent work (Black et al., 2025a; Bjorck et al., 2025a) has shifted toward hybrid architectures in which diffusion-based action heads are conditioned on VLM representations. Despite these advances, current VLAs predominantly rely on visual and linguistic modalities, overlooking physical feedback ( e.g., tactile, force, and torque) that is critical for contact-rich manipulation. In this work, we focus on diffusion-based VLAs and demonstrate how our approach enables seamless integration of diverse physical sensory signals into their action generation process. 

Policy learning with physical feedback. Recently, sev-eral approaches have attempted to extend pretrained VLAs with additional physical sensory modalities, demonstrat-ing their effectiveness on contact-rich manipulation tasks. Such physical signals can be broadly categorized into two types based on their measurement location: (a) end-effector sensing (e.g., tactile or force), and (b) joint-level sensing 

(e.g., torque). For end-effector sensing, Yu et al. (2025); Huang et al. (2025b); Bi et al. (2025); Cheng et al. (2025); Adeniji et al. (2025) leverage external tactile or force sen-sors mounted on the gripper or hand, while Zhang et al. (2025a) employs high-resolution visuo-tactile sensors to support tasks such as insertion, grasping, and fabric ma-nipulation. For joint-level sensing, Zhang et al. (2025b) utilizes torque feedback from arm joints to enable com-pliant and contact-aware control. While these specialized approaches demonstrate strong task-specific performance, they typically focus on a single sensing modality. Multimodal architectures. Many recent efforts have at-tempted to design an architecture to incorporate a diverse range of modalities. In particular, the success of VLMs has been largely driven by advances in multimodal fusion architectures: early approaches (Nagrani et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2020) employed cross-attention mechanisms to align visual and textual representations, while more recent work has explored richer fusion strategies, including perceiver-based architectures (Jaegle et al., 2021), modality-specific adapters (Li et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024), and unified tokenization schemes (Lu et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2024). These methods demonstrate that care-fully designed fusion mechanisms can effectively model heterogeneous modalities with different dimensionalities and semantic structures. In the robotics domain, recent VLAs (Black et al., 2025b; Bjorck et al., 2025b) have suc-cessfully adapted such vision-language fusion techniques to incorporate action prediction. However, extending these architectures to physical sensing modalities poses additional challenges, as such signals differ fundamentally from visual and linguistic inputs in temporal structure, scale, and seman-tics. Our work draws inspiration from multimodal fusion in VLMs, but adapts it specifically to the action prediction setting, introducing a modular architecture that enables pre-trained VLAs to integrate diverse physical sensory signals while preserving their action priors.

3.2. Two-Stage Training Strategy 

We employ a two-stage training scheme designed to preserve pretrained knowledge while enabling effective learning of physical sensory representations. 

Stage 1: Physical alignment. We freeze all parameters of the pretrained action expert module Aψ and train only the modular sensory streams {A ϕi }Ni=1 , which are initialized from scratch. This allows the new streams to learn mean-ingful representations of physical interactions ( e.g., tactile contacts, force feedback, and joint torques) and establish cross-modal correspondences through joint self-attention, without disrupting the pretrained action policy. 

Stage 2: Joint fine-tuning. After initializing the modular sensory streams {A ϕi }Ni=1 , we unfreeze the action expert module Aψ and perform joint fine-tuning. This enables the action stream to incorporate physical feedback M while the sensory streams adapt to task-specific requirements, allowing both streams to learn coordinated behaviors for physically-aware manipulation. We find this staged train-ing crucial for stability, as directly performing end-to-end training from scratch leads to optimization difficulties due to gradient interference between the randomly initialized sensory streams {A ϕi }Ni=1 and pretrained action stream Aψ

Hardware setup. We construct a single-arm gripper plat-form, which consists of a 7-DoF Franka Research 3 robotic arm equipped with a Robotiq 2F-85 gripper, following the DROID (Khazatsky et al., 2024) setup. Here, we mount an AnySkin (Bhirangi et al., 2025) tactile sensor on the gripper, while the robot arm itself supports torque sensing at each joint. Specifically, for tactile signals, each AnySkin sensor provides a 15-dimensional feature vector, obtained from five sensing units that each measure a 3-dimensional force vector ( i.e., x, y, z). Since we attach one AnySkin sensor to each gripper finger, our tactile sensing system provides a 30-dimensional observation vector at each timestep. For the torque signals, the robot provides a 7-dimensional vector (one scalar torque value per joint) measured in Newton-meters. Further specifications of the physical sensory signal, are provided in Appendix A.2. 

Baselines. To demonstrate the generality of our approach, we apply MoSS to two state-of-the-art VLAs: π0 (Black et al., 2025b) and GR00T N1.5 (Bjorck et al., 2025a). We then compare MoSS with several modality-augmented VLA baselines: (i) Tactile-VLA (Huang et al., 2025b) incorpo-rates tactile signals on the gripper as additional input tokens to the VLM backbone; (ii) ForceVLA (Yu et al., 2025) em-ploys an MoE-based encoder to fuse 3-axis force signals on the gripper with VLM features and conditions the action expert module on them; (iii) TA-VLA (Zhang et al., 2025b) injects torque signals from robotic arm joints directly into the action expert module, while training the policy to predict future torque signals as an auxiliary task. For each method, we adapt it to use the physical sensory signals available on our hardware platforms.

However, when extend-ing GR00T N1.5 with Tactile-VLA, we also fine-tune the VLM, as Tactile-VLA integrates tactile signals directly into the VLM, requiring the backbone to adapt to this unseen modality introduced during training. 4.2. Main Results 

We provide quantitative results in Table 1, and the quali-tative results of rollout examples in Figure 4. We provide more results, including evaluation on simulated manipula-tion benchmarks, in Section C. In Table 1, we first observe that base VLAs ( i.e., fine-tuned VLAs without physical sensing) struggle on these precision manipulation tasks, achieving average success rates of 20.8% and 26.1% for GR00T N1.5 and π0, respec-tively. This indicates that our tasks require fine-grained force control, highlighting the difficulty of performing them successfully with vision-and-language-only policies. This confirms our hypothesis that vision and language alone are insufficient for tasks requiring fine-grained force control. In contrast, we find that our framework, MoSS, achieves substantial improvements across all tasks and base mod-els. For example, for GR00T N1.5, we achieve an average improvement of 28.2% over the base model, with partic-ularly strong gains on Unstack Cup (+37.5%) and Board Erase (+29.2%). Similar improvements are observed on π0,demonstrating that our approach generalizes across different VLA architectures. We also find that MoSS consistently out-performs several physical modality-augmented VLA base-lines. For example, on the PnP Egg task, GR00T N1.5 + MoSS with tactile signals achieves 66.7%, but GR00T N1.5 + ForceVLA gets 54.2%. This highlights our design that maintains a separate processing stream with controlled cross-modal joint self-attention, which enables effective integration of physical sensing without corrupting action representation. Moreover, MoSS consistently improves as we add additional physical sensor modalities, demonstrat-ing that MoSS is scalable framework to adapt VLAs with multiple physical modalities.

## 5. Conclusion 

In this work, we have presented MoSS, a scalable modu-lar framework that adapts Vision-Language-Action models (VLAs) to leverage multiple sensory signals for action pre-diction. By extending the action experts with new streams, MoSS can incorporate various modalities without corrupting the pretrained features. Our experiments show that MoSS improves performance on contact-rich, real-world manipu-lation tasks, and the performance continues to improve as more physical sensory modalities are incorporated. ## A. Experimental Details 

A.1. Task Specifications. 

We collected 100 datasets per task with extensive variation across multiple dimensions to ensure robust generalization and avoid memorization. We use the third-person view camera and wrist camera of 720 ×1280 resolution without depth. We use the visual observation after resizing it to 224 ×224 resolution. We use absolute joint position as action mode. We describe the detailed scenarios as follows: 

Unstack Cup: The task required lifting only the top red cup from a stack. To prevent gripper position memorization, we varied the cup sizes, using both large and small cups in different configurations. In particular, we evaluate each model over 24 trials, consisting of 12 trials with a large cup and 12 trials with a small cup. We use the instruction “ Pick up the top red cup from the stack and place it next to the blue cup. ” for this task. 

PnP Egg: The egg was placed in various positions across the workspace to evaluate spatial generalization. We evaluate each model over 24 trials. We use the instruction “ Pick up the egg and place it in the gold bowl. ” for this task. 

Board Erase: The whiteboard was positioned at three different heights to test height adaptability. We varied the eraser colors (red and grey) with slight differences in object size, and used markers of different colors (blue and black). Evaluations were conducted across diverse environmental conditions to assess robustness. In particular, we evaluate each model over 24 trials, consisting of one trial per combination of (height, eraser color, object size, different color). We use the instruction “Use the (red/grey) eraser to clean the whiteboard. ” for this task. 

Plug Insertion: We used chargers of three different colors (yellow, black, and white) for color and object generalization, with target insertion positions varied across trials. We evaluate each model over 24 trials, consisting of 8 trials per charger. We use the instruction “ Pick up the (yellow/white/black) charger and plug it into the socket. ” for this task. 11 Modular Sensory Stream for Integrating Physical Feedback in Vision-Language-Action Models
